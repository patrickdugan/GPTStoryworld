# Historical Alliance Lessons for 3-Agent Reasoning

## Case: U.S. balancing India and Pakistan in Cold War and 1971 crisis

This case is useful for modeling why triadic reassurance can fail when commitments are not jointly compatible.

### Key dates (for concrete timeline features)

- April 2, 1954: Turkey-Pakistan Agreement for Friendly Cooperation signed.
- May 19, 1954: U.S.-Pakistan Mutual Defense Assistance Agreement signed in Karachi.
- September 8, 1954: SEATO treaty signed (Pakistan included); entered into force February 19, 1955.
- August 9, 1971: India-USSR Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation signed.
- 1971 South Asia War period: U.S. policy documents describe a "tilt" toward Pakistan.

### Why "be nice to both" can fail in this structure

In a 3-agent rivalry (U.S., India, Pakistan), policy tried to preserve ties with both sides while also making security commitments.
When side-payments and assurances are perceived as asymmetric, each regional actor updates:

- direct pValues downward ("their loyalty to us is weaker than stated"),
- p2Values downward ("they think we are expendable"),
- betrayal priors upward.

The failure mode is not only conflicting interests. It is conflicting *belief updates* across all three edges.

## Death-ground as a game-theoretic state

"Death-ground" (no safe retreat) changes payoff shape.

- In normal regimes, betrayal can look attractive because future repair is possible.
- In death-ground regimes, commitment and deterrence can dominate because credibility rises when retreat options collapse.

Model implication:
- Raise risk_tolerance and resolve weights.
- Raise value of credible commitments.
- Increase penalty for ambiguous signals (ambiguity can be fatal under existential pressure).

## Storyworld implementation guidance

1. Add explicit irreversible commitment options in late-branch encounters.
2. Add witness reactions that update p2Values across third-party observers.
3. In death-ground branches, make isolation less viable and coalition clarity more valuable.
4. Make betrayal very high short-term utility but sharply penalize medium-horizon trust manifold dimensions.
